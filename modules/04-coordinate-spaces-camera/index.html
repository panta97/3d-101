Coordinate Spaces & the Camera

Model, world and view space; change of basis; the cheap rigid inverse; building lookAt.

Module 3 left two threads dangling, and both are about the same object: the camera. You've been dragging an orbit camera around every diagram for three modules, and we kept promising it was "just a matrix." It is. A camera is nothing more exotic than a coordinate frame that happens to have a lens — and "render the world from the camera's point of view" is nothing more than "rewrite every point in the camera's coordinates."

So this module is really about one idea wearing several hats: the same point has different coordinates in different frames, and a matrix is the dictionary that translates between them. Model space, world space, view space — three frames, two dictionaries. You'll build the dictionaries, discover that one of them (the view matrix) is an inverse, panic briefly about inverting a 4×4, and then learn the shortcut that makes it almost free. By the end you'll have written lookAt — the function at the heart of every camera in every engine — and flown it through a scene.

4.1 A point is nothing without a frame

Quick: where is $(3, 0, 0)$? You can't answer, and that's the whole lesson. Three units along which x-axis, measured from whose origin? Coordinates are not positions — they're instructions relative to a frame: an origin plus three axes. Change the frame and the same physical point gets new numbers, the same numbers land on a new point.

3D engines juggle three frames constantly. A model is authored in model space (a.k.a. local space) — the teapot's spout is at some fixed local coordinate the artist set, with the origin wherever they dropped the pivot. Drop that teapot into a scene and it gains a position, rotation and scale in world space, the shared stage every object stands on. And to draw it, you'll re-express everything one more time in view space — the world as measured from the camera's eye. Model → world → view: each arrow is a change of frame, and you already built the first one.

A frame is a matrix, and you wrote the constructor in Module 3 without knowing it: pack the three axes into columns 0–2 and the origin into column 3, and you have a model→world matrix. Feed it local coordinates, transformPoint3 hands back world coordinates. The TRS matrix from 3.7 was exactly this — a frame whose axes happen to be a rotated, scaled basis.

$$ M_{\text{frame}} = \begin{bmatrix} \,\hat{x} & \hat{y} & \hat{z} & o\, \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_x & y_x & z_x & o_x \\ x_y & y_y & z_y & o_y \\ x_z & y_z & z_z & o_z \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Your frameMatrix(x, y, z, o) below just drops four vectors into a 4×4. Trivial to write — but it nails down the mental model the rest of the module leans on: the columns of any transform are where its frame's axes point, and the last column is where its origin sits. Read a matrix that way and it stops being a grid of numbers and becomes a little coordinate system you can see.

Every prop, character and bullet in a game scene is an frameMatrix the engine rebuilds each frame from the object's transform. "Attach the sword to the hand bone" is literally "use the hand's frame matrix as the sword's parent" — the capstone of Module 3, seen from the other side.

4.2 Change of basis: reading a point in another frame

frameMatrix goes local → world. Now run it backwards: given a point's world coordinates, what are its coordinates in some other frame? This is the operation a camera performs on the entire world, so it's worth doing slowly and by hand once.

For a frame that's orthonormal — axes that are unit length and mutually perpendicular, which every rotation gives you — the answer is gorgeous. Slide the point so the frame's origin becomes your origin (subtract $o$), then ask "how far along each axis?" And "how far along a unit vector" is a question you answered in Module 2: it's the dot product.

$$ p_{\text{local}} = \big(\, (p - o)\cdot\hat{x},\;\; (p - o)\cdot\hat{y},\;\; (p - o)\cdot\hat{z} \,\big) $$

Change of basis into an orthonormal frame is three dot products. No matrix inverse, no solving equations — projection onto each axis reads off that coordinate directly. This is why orthonormal frames are worth their weight in gold, and why cameras are built from them.

Sanity check the round trip: frameMatrix rebuilds a point as $o + l_x\hat{x} + l_y\hat{y} + l_z\hat{z}$, and worldToLocal recovers $(l_x, l_y, l_z)$ from the point. They undo each other exactly — the widget draws that reconstruction as a colored staircase that should land dead on the target point. (When the axes are not orthonormal the dot-product trick fails and you really do need a matrix inverse — but we'll never let our frames get into that state.)

The dot-product shortcut is a privilege, not a right: it holds only when the axes are orthonormal. Feed worldToLocal a frame with non-perpendicular or non-unit axes and the numbers it returns are quietly wrong. The next section is how you guarantee you never hand it a bad frame.

4.3 Manufacturing a frame: the orthonormal basis

Here's the practical problem. You want a camera at the eye, looking at a target, with "up" roughly toward the sky. That gives you a forward direction (target minus eye) and a rough up — but those two are almost never perpendicular, and forward probably isn't unit length. You can't build an orthonormal frame from sloppy inputs by wishing. You build it with Gram-Schmidt, which here is just two cross products.

Watch the logic. cross always returns a vector perpendicular to both its inputs, so $\hat{r} = \text{normalize}(\hat{f} \times \text{up})$ is guaranteed perpendicular to forward — that's your right axis, and the sloppy "up" only had to point roughly the right way to pin down which side is right. Then rebuild a clean up as $\hat{u} = \hat{r} \times \hat{f}$: perpendicular to both, and automatically unit length because it's the cross of two perpendicular unit vectors.

$$ \hat{f} = \frac{\text{fwd}}{\lVert \text{fwd} \rVert}, \qquad \hat{r} = \frac{\hat{f} \times \text{up}}{\lVert \hat{f} \times \text{up} \rVert}, \qquad \hat{u} = \hat{r} \times \hat{f} $$

Take whatever forward you're given, manufacture a perpendicular right with one cross, then manufacture a perpendicular up with a second cross. Three clean, unit, mutually perpendicular axes out of two messy inputs — that's the entire trick behind every "look in this direction" function in graphics.

Order and handedness matter: cross(fwd, up) versus cross(up, fwd) flip right into left, and a left-handed camera frame mirror-images your whole render. The widget reports every dot product (should be $\approx 0$) and every length (should be $\approx 1$) so you can see the frame is genuinely orthonormal, not just close.

One degenerate case lurks: if forward is parallel to up, then $\hat{f} \times \text{up}$ is the zero vector and normalize can't save it — the frame collapses. It's why you can't look straight up with a $(0,1,0)$ up vector, and why the orbit cameras in this course clamp pitch just shy of vertical. Real engines special-case it; we sidestep it.

4.4 lookAt, part 1: placing the camera

Now assemble. A camera is a framed object like any other, so its model→world matrix — cameraToWorld — is just frameMatrix fed the camera's frame: the orthonormal basis from 4.3, with the eye as the origin.

There's exactly one convention to swallow, and it's the source of a thousand sign-flip bugs. By long-standing tradition (OpenGL, WebGL, and this course), a camera in its own space sits at the origin and looks down its $-z$ axis. So the camera's "forward" — toward the target — is the $-z$ direction, which means the frame's third column, its actual z-axis, points backward, away from what you're looking at. Columns 0, 1, 2 are therefore right, up, and back $= -\hat{f}$:

$$ \text{cameraToWorld} = \begin{bmatrix} \hat{r} & \hat{u} & -\hat{f} & \text{eye} \\ \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \hat{f} = \text{normalize(target} - \text{eye)} $$

The camera looks down $-z$. Internalize that one fact and the rest of graphics stops fighting you: the back axis is $+z$, the projection in Module 5 divides by $-z$, and "in front of the camera" means "negative z." Memorize the convention, not the formula.

The widget reads the eye, the red/green/blue axes, and the little view frustum straight out of the matrix you build — column 3 is the eye, columns 0–2 are the axes, and the frustum opens along $-z$ toward the target. Drag the eye around and the camera body follows, because the body is the matrix.

This is the matrix that mounts a camera to a tripod, a car bumper, or a character's eyes. A first-person camera is cameraToWorld with the eye and forward yanked from the player every frame; a cutscene camera is the same matrix keyframed by an animator.

4.5 lookAt, part 2: the cheap rigid inverse

Here's the twist that catches everyone. cameraToWorld takes camera-space points out into the world — but rendering needs the opposite: take world points into the camera. That's the inverse, and it has a name you'll see all over graphics code: the view matrix. So the view matrix is $\text{cameraToWorld}^{-1}$, and your stomach drops, because inverting a general 4×4 is a miserable 16-term cofactor slog you do not want to write or run per frame.

You don't have to — and this is the beautiful shortcut Module 3 promised. The camera matrix isn't general. It's rigid: a rotation $R$ (orthonormal — its columns are perpendicular unit vectors) followed by a translation $t$. And a rigid matrix inverts almost for free, because an orthonormal matrix's inverse is its transpose: $R^{-1} = R^{\mathsf{T}}$. No determinant, no division — just reflect across the diagonal.

$$ M = \begin{bmatrix} R & t \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad M^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} R^{\mathsf{T}} & -R^{\mathsf{T}}t \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

For a rigid transform, inverse = transpose the rotation, and re-aim the translation as $-R^{\mathsf{T}}t$. It's $O(1)$, it's exact (no floating-point division to drift), and it's why no engine ever calls a general matrix inverse to build a view matrix.

Why $-R^{\mathsf{T}}t$ and not just $-t$? Because undoing $M$ means undoing the translation and then the rotation, in that reversed order: $M^{-1} = R^{-1}T^{-1}$. The $-R^{\mathsf{T}}t$ column is exactly "slide back by $-t$, then rotate that offset by $R^{\mathsf{T}}$." In the homogeneous form, $R^{\mathsf{T}}$ in the upper-left and $-R^{\mathsf{T}}t$ in the last column do both jobs in one matrix. (Convince yourself: column $i$ of $R^{\mathsf{T}}t$ is the dot of $R$'s $i$-th column with $t$ — which is why the formula reads off $m_0, m_1, m_2$ for the first entry, not $m_0, m_4, m_8$.)

Build invertRigid, then lookAt is a one-liner: $\text{lookAt} = \text{invertRigid}(\text{cameraToWorld})$. The widget scrambles a cube with a rigid $M$ and asks your inverse to put it back; the readout multiplies $M^{-1}M$ and shows it collapsing to the identity. If your cube doesn't come home, you transposed wrong or forgot the $-R^{\mathsf{T}}t$ re-aim.

The transpose shortcut works only while the rotation stays orthonormal. The moment a non-uniform scale sneaks into your "rigid" matrix (Module 3's normals warning, again), the columns stop being unit length, the transpose stops being the inverse, and your camera silently skews. View matrices are rotation-and-translation only — keep scale out.

★ Capstone: fly the camera

Time to collapse the whole module into one chain. A vertex starts in model space, gets placed in the world by its model matrix, and lands in view space via your view matrix — and because chains read right-to-left, the single matrix that does all of it at once is $\text{modelView} = \text{lookAt} \cdot \text{model}$.

Model first, view second: $\text{view} \cdot \text{model}$. The object's own transform runs before the camera's, exactly as you'd expect — put the teapot on the table, then decide where you're standing to look at it. Every vertex in every frame of every 3D game flows through some version of this product.

Your modelView below builds that matrix, and the widget makes it literal. The left panel is a free orbit view of the world with your camera drawn as a frustum — that's the camera you're flying. The right panel renders the same world as that camera sees it, by pushing every object through your modelView. Fly the camera with the sliders and the two panels stay locked together: turn left in the world, the world swings right through the lens. Get lookAt wrong and the right panel lurches the wrong way while the frustum on the left stays honest — the cleanest bug-finder in the course.

Full honesty about the right panel: turning view-space coordinates into a flat image still needs the perspective divide — the "things farther away look smaller" squish. That last black box is the only piece of the rendering pipeline you haven't built yourself, and it's the entire subject of Module 5. Everything else on screen — every axis, every cube, every line of the world-to-camera transform — is now your code.

You've reached a milestone the course has been building toward since Module 1: the camera that draws these diagrams is no longer a black box you were promised would be explained. You wrote it. Module 5 takes the last step — the projection — and then the renderer drawing every picture on this page is made entirely of functions you built.

One thread forward. Your modelView hands view space to a projection you haven't written — the perspective divide that maps a 3D frustum onto a 2D rectangle and makes distance shrink things.
