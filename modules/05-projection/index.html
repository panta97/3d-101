Projection: 3D onto the Screen

The perspective divide, the projection matrix, normalized device coordinates, the viewport transform — project3() finally demystified.

For four modules a single function has done all the heavy lifting and refused to explain itself. project3() took a 3D point and handed back a pixel, and every diagram you have ever dragged was secretly calling it. Module 4 ended one step away: you built the whole model → world → view chain yourself, and the only black box left was the last squish — the one that makes far things small and turns a 3D world into a flat image. This module is that squish.

It comes apart into surprisingly few pieces. There is one genuinely new idea — dividing by depth — and then a lot of bookkeeping to do that division with a matrix and map the result onto a rectangle of pixels. By the end you will have rebuilt project3() from scratch, and the renderer drawing every picture on every page of this course will be made entirely of functions you wrote.

5.1 The perspective divide

Hold a finger up close and it blots out a building. Perspective is just the observation that the same object covers less of your view the farther away it sits — and the math is embarrassingly simple. Put the eye at the origin looking down $-z$ (the convention from Module 4). A point sits at camera-space $(x, y, z)$, at depth $d = -z$ in front of you. Drop an image plane a distance $f$ (the focal length) ahead. The ray from the point back to the eye pierces that plane, and similar triangles read off where:

$$ x' = f\,\frac{x}{-z}, \qquad y' = f\,\frac{y}{-z} $$

Projection is division by depth. That single $/(-z)$ is the entire reason distant things look small — double the depth, halve the image. Everything else in this module is machinery for doing that one division cleanly and turning the result into pixels.

Your projectPinhole below is that formula and nothing more. The widget draws the eye, the image plane, and your point; the red dot is where your function lands it. Slide the point deeper and watch it shrink toward the center; the dot must stay glued to the sight ray, because that ray is the set of all points that project to the same spot.

Depth is $-z$, not $z$. The camera looks down $-z$, so a visible point has a negative $z$ and a positive depth. Divide by $z$ instead of $-z$ and your whole image flips through the origin — the single most common sign bug in graphics.

5.2 The projection matrix

You divide by depth in one line — so why does every engine wrap projection in a 4×4 matrix? Because the rest of the pipeline is matrices, and a matrix can be premultiplied into the model-view product so a vertex takes one transform, not a transform followed by a special-case divide. But there is a catch you can already feel: a matrix multiply is linear — it can add and scale coordinates, but it cannot divide one coordinate by another. So how can a matrix do the perspective divide?

It doesn't — it defers it. The projection matrix is built so the output's fourth coordinate $w$ comes out equal to $-z$ (the depth). The division by $w$ happens in the next step, on its own. The matrix's only job is to load the right number into $w$ and pre-scale $x, y, z$ so that after the divide everything lands in a tidy cube.

That tidy cube is the point. The matrix maps the camera's view frustum — the truncated pyramid of everything it can see, bounded by a field of view and the near/far planes — onto a canonical $[-1, 1]^3$ box. Here is the standard perspective matrix, with $f = 1/\tan(\text{fovY}/2)$:

$$ P = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{f}{\text{aspect}} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\[4pt] 0 & f & 0 & 0 \\[4pt] 0 & 0 & \dfrac{\text{far}+\text{near}}{\text{near}-\text{far}} & \dfrac{2\,\text{far}\,\text{near}}{\text{near}-\text{far}} \\[4pt] 0 & 0 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

Read the rows. The top two scale $x$ and $y$ by the focal length (and undo the aspect ratio on $x$ so a wide canvas doesn't stretch the picture). The third row remaps depth into the $[-1, 1]$ range. And the bottom row is the whole trick: $(0, 0, -1, 0)$ means the output $w = -z$. That $-1$ is the most important number in the matrix.

Storage reminder: this is column-major, like every Mat4 since Module 3. The $-1$ that copies $-z$ into $w$ lives at index m[11], and the depth-remap terms are m[10] and m[14]. The starter spells out every index so you can drop the values straight in.

Change the field of view on a real camera and you get a zoom lens; change it here and you are editing f in this exact matrix. The "dolly zoom" that makes hallways stretch in thrillers is an animator sliding fovY and the eye in opposite directions, frame by frame.

5.3 Clip space and the perspective divide

Now apply the matrix. Back in Module 3 you wrote transformPoint3, and it quietly cheated: it computed three output coordinates and ignored the matrix's bottom row, because for every transform you had built so far the fourth coordinate $w$ was always $1$. The projection matrix breaks that assumption on purpose — it puts $-z$ in the bottom row — so you need the honest version that keeps $w$.

$$ \begin{bmatrix} x_c \\ y_c \\ z_c \\ w_c \end{bmatrix} = P \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad w_c = -z $$

The result $(x_c, y_c, z_c, w_c)$ lives in clip space — named because the GPU clips triangles against the cube here, while $w$ is still around to make the math linear. Then comes the moment everything has been building toward, the perspective divide: divide all four coordinates through by $w$.

$$ \text{NDC} = \left( \frac{x_c}{w_c},\; \frac{y_c}{w_c},\; \frac{z_c}{w_c} \right) $$

The divide by $w$ is the divide by depth from 5.1 — you just routed the depth through $w$ so a linear matrix could carry it this far. After it, every on-screen point lives in normalized device coordinates: a resolution-independent $[-1, 1]^3$ cube, $-1$ at left/bottom/near, $+1$ at right/top/far.

Build transformPoint4 (transformPoint3 with the bottom row restored) and perspectiveDivide (three divisions). The widget shows a point in view space on the left and its NDC landing spot on the right; the readout prints the full clip-space 4-vector so you can watch $w$ track the depth and the divide squeeze deeper points harder toward the center.

5.4 The viewport transform: NDC to pixels

NDC is almost a picture — it just doesn't know your canvas. It's a $2 \times 2$ square centered on the origin; your canvas is a width × height rectangle whose origin is the top-left corner and whose $y$ grows downward. The viewport transform is the last, humblest step: remap each axis and flip $y$.

$$ x_{\text{px}} = \frac{x_{\text{ndc}} + 1}{2}\,\text{width}, \qquad y_{\text{px}} = \frac{1 - y_{\text{ndc}}}{2}\,\text{height} $$

The $y$-flip catches everyone. Math and NDC put $+y$ up; screens and canvases put $+y$ down. Forget the $(1 - y_{\text{ndc}})$ and your render is upside-down — perfectly rendered, then mirrored top-to-bottom. It is the bug that makes a debugged scene look haunted.

Your viewport is two lines, but it is where the whole pipeline finally touches the screen. The widget treats the entire canvas as the viewport: drag anywhere, watch the spot reported as an NDC coordinate, and see your function place the pixel. The corner labels make the flip concrete — NDC's top-left maps to pixel $(0, 0)$, its bottom-left to $(0, \text{height})$.

5.5 Orthographic: projection without the divide

Not every projection foreshortens. CAD drawings, architectural blueprints, and isometric strategy games deliberately throw perspective away so that a one-meter beam is one meter long wherever it sits — measurable, comparable, honest about size. That's orthographic projection, and the contrast pins down exactly what perspective was doing.

Orthographic keeps $w = 1$. Its bottom row is the ordinary $(0, 0, 0, 1)$, so the perspective divide divides by one and changes nothing. No depth in the denominator means no shrinking with distance — parallel lines stay parallel forever.

$$ O = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{2}{\text{width}} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\[4pt] 0 & \dfrac{2}{\text{height}} & 0 & 0 \\[4pt] 0 & 0 & \dfrac{-2}{\text{far}-\text{near}} & -\dfrac{\text{far}+\text{near}}{\text{far}-\text{near}} \\[4pt] 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

It's the same job as perspective — map a viewing volume onto the NDC cube — but the volume is now a box, not a frustum, so the matrix is pure scale-and-shift with no $-1$ in the $w$ row. The widget renders one row of identical cubes twice: through a perspective matrix on the left (the far ones shrink) and through your orthographic on the right (they stay dead equal). Seeing both at once is the clearest possible statement of what that single division buys you.

★ Capstone: project3() un-boxed

Everything assembles into the function the course has leaned on since Module 1. A vertex starts in model space and rides a single matrix — projection · view · model — into clip space; you keep its $w$, divide to NDC, and map to a pixel. That's project3(), and now you write it:

$$ \text{pixel} = \text{viewport}\Big( \text{perspectiveDivide}\big( \, (P \cdot V \cdot M)\,\mathbf{p}\, \big) \Big) $$

One matrix, one divide, one remap — with a guard. Points at or behind the eye have $w \le 0$ and no honest place on screen, so project3 returns null for them and the renderer simply skips those edges. That cull is why a wall behind the camera doesn't smear across your view.

The widget renders a real scene of cubes entirely through your project3: every edge, every grid line, every axis is your pipeline running on live data. Orbit the camera, widen the field of view, and the picture responds because the math is yours — the perspective divide you derived in 5.1, the matrix from 5.2, the clip-to-NDC of 5.3, the viewport of 5.4, all firing at once.

This is the last black box gone. The orbit camera that drew the vectors in Module 1, the planes in Module 2, the matrices in Module 3, the lookAt frustum in Module 4 — all of it was project3(), and you have now built every line of it. From here on the course adds new powers (triangles, light, textures, ray tracing), but the machine that turns a 3D point into a pixel on your screen is, start to finish, your code.

One thread forward. You can now place a 3D point precisely on the screen — but a scene is made of triangles, not points, and the screen is made of pixels you have to fill.
