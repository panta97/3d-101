Dot, Cross, and Planes

Two ways to multiply vectors — and the geometry empire built on them.

Module 1 ended on a loose thread. You can add vectors, subtract them, scale them by a number, measure them, normalize them, lerp them — but the one arithmetic move we never made was multiplying two vectors by each other. What should a * b even mean when both sides have a direction?

It turns out there are two answers worth having, and they answer two different questions. The dot product takes two vectors and hands back a number: how much do these two agree? The cross product takes two vectors and hands back a vector: what is perpendicular to both? Between them they make nearly every geometric decision a game or renderer ever makes: can the guard see you, which way does this surface face, is that tree on screen, where does the laser go next.

By the end of this module you'll have written all of it yourself — the stealth-game FOV check, the wall-slide from Quake, frustum culling, the mirror-bounce formula — and used nothing but those pieces to light a sphere, one dot product per pixel.

2.1 The dot product: an alignment meter

The recipe could not be plainer: multiply matching components, then add everything up. For 2D vectors $a$ and $b$,

$$ a \cdot b \;=\; a_x b_x + a_y b_y $$

and dot3 is the same idea with a $+\,a_z b_z$ tacked on. Two multiplies and an add — no square roots, no trig, the cheapest function in this course. The shock is how much geometry hides in it, because the same number also equals $a \cdot b = |a|\,|b|\cos\theta$, where $|a|$ and $|b|$ are the lengths you wrote in Module 1 and $\theta$ is the angle between the two vectors laid tail to tail. Cosine is positive for angles under 90°, zero at exactly 90°, negative beyond — so the sign of one cheap number tells you whether two directions agree, are perpendicular, or oppose each other, and the magnitude tells you by how much.

dot(a, b) answers one question — how much does a go in b's direction? Positive with, zero perpendicular, negative against: a one-number summary of agreement.

And here's the bridge to what you already own: feed a vector to itself and you get $v \cdot v = v_x^2 + v_y^2$ — that is lengthSq(v), character for character. You wrote your first dot product in Module 1 without being told; length is just self-dot, square-rooted.

Lambert's lighting law — max(0, dot(n, L)), surface normal against light direction — is arguably the most-executed math expression in computer graphics, run for every pixel of every lit surface of every frame. Dark Souls decides a backstab with roughly dot(victimFwd, attackerFwd) > 0.7 — you and your victim facing the same way. And backface culling drops half of every model's triangles with one dot against the view direction before a single pixel is shaded.

2.2 Projection: shadows and decomposition

When $b$ has length 1 (write it $\hat b$), the geometric formula collapses: $a \cdot \hat b = |a|\cos\theta$, which is exactly the length of $a$'s shadow on $\hat b$'s line — a signed coordinate along that direction. Dot with a unit vector reads off a coordinate. To turn that shadow back into a vector, rebuild it: measure, then scale the direction by the measurement — scale(b̂, dot(a, b̂)).

For a $b$ of any length you could normalize first, but a little algebra dodges the square root entirely: project(a, b) = scale(b, dot(a, b) / lengthSq(b)). The division by lengthSq(b) cancels $b$'s length out twice — once for the measuring, once for the rebuilding — so only $b$'s direction matters, as it should. (Module 1's zero policy applies: project(a, (0,0)) returns (0,0), never NaN.)

Whatever projection didn't capture is the rejection: reject(a, b) = sub(a, project(a, b)), the part of $a$ perpendicular to $b$. Project plus reject rebuilds $a$ exactly — every vector splits cleanly into along + across.

Dot with a unit vector reads off a coordinate along that direction; projection is "measure, then rebuild" — any vector splits into along + across, and physics, lighting and cameras all run on that split.

velocity -= n * dot(velocity, n) — subtract the into-the-wall part, keep the along-the-wall part — is the soul of Quake's player movement, and of every FPS since. That smooth glide when you strafe into a wall at an angle? You just wrote it. It's the rejection, spelled differently.

2.3 Cosine space: angles without acos

Sometimes you genuinely need the angle: angleBetween(a, b) normalizes both sides (dividing the dot by $|a|\,|b|$), then applies Math.acos to the cosine. But there's a landmine. Mathematically that cosine lives in $[-1, 1]$; in floating point, two parallel vectors can produce $1.0000000000000002$ — and acos of that is NaN, which then infects everything it touches. The fix is one line: clamp the cosine to $[-1, 1]$ before the call. Always.

Mostly, though, you don't need the angle at all — you need a comparison against a threshold, and you can do that entirely in cosine space. "Is the target within my vision cone?" means the angle between my facing and the direction to the target is at most half the cone's width: $\theta \le \mathrm{fov}/2$. Take the cosine of both sides — remembering cosine decreases on $[0, \pi]$, so the inequality flips — and the test becomes dot(facing normalized, toTarget normalized) >= Math.cos(fov / 2). The right side is a constant you hoist out of the loop; the hot path is a subtract, a couple of normalizes, and a dot. That's isInFov(guardPos, facing, targetPos, fov) — and don't trust facing to arrive unit-length; normalize it, or a long facing vector inflates the dot until the guard sees everything.

Don't compute the angle — compare cosines: dot(â, b̂) >= cos(threshold) does the FOV test with zero trig in the hot loop; and when you must acos, clamp first, because floats will hand you 1.0000000000000002 and acos will hand you NaN.

The clamp isn't paranoia. angleBetween between a vector and a float-dirty copy of itself is the test below that breaks naive implementations — and in production it breaks them rarely, intermittently, and only in release builds, which is so much worse.

2.4 The cross product: manufacturing perpendicularity

The dot product collapses two vectors into a number. Its sibling builds a new vector. For 3D vectors $a$ and $b$:

$$ a \times b = \big(\,a_y b_z - a_z b_y,\;\; a_z b_x - a_x b_z,\;\; a_x b_y - a_y b_x\,\big) $$

Each component skips its own axis and cross-multiplies the other two. The result is perpendicular to both inputs — in our right-handed world, cross(x̂, ŷ) is exactly $\hat z$ (curl your right hand's fingers from $a$ toward $b$; your thumb is the answer). Two bonuses ride along: the length $|a \times b| = |a|\,|b|\sin\theta$ is the area of the parallelogram the two vectors span, and swapping the arguments flips the result — cross(b, a) = neg(cross(a, b)). That anticommutativity isn't a quirk to memorize; it's how geometry encodes winding order. Parallel inputs pin down no unique perpendicular, so the cross of parallel vectors collapses to the zero vector — keep that failure mode in mind.

cross(a, b) is the only vector guaranteed perpendicular to both — it's where every surface normal comes from — its length bonus-reports the parallelogram's area, and swapping arguments flips it: order is meaning.

2D has a shadow of this. Embed two 2D vectors at $z = 0$ and only the $z$ component of their cross survives: cross2(a, b) = a.x * b.y - a.y * b.x, a single number whose sign says whether $b$ is to the left (+) or right (−) of $a$. File that away: in Module 6 that little sign test rasterizes triangles. Today it steers a car.

n = normalize3(cross(sub3(b, a), sub3(c, a))) — two triangle edges in, unit normal out — is where every surface normal in every mesh you've ever seen comes from. Lighting, collision response, decals, foot placement: all downstream of one cross product per triangle.

The legendary NaN camera: build a camera basis with cross(forward, up) and let the player look straight up — forward and up go parallel, the cross collapses to zero, normalize hits the zero policy (or worse, NaN in engines without one), and the screen goes black or starts spinning. The standard fix: when |dot(forward, up)| creeps near 1, swap in a fallback up axis. Every engine has this if-statement; now you know why.

2.5 Planes: a dot-product threshold

Here is the entire definition of a plane in this course: a unit normal $n$ and a number $d$. The plane is every point $p$ where $n \cdot p = d$ — that is, every point whose coordinate along $n$ reads exactly $d$. (That's Section 2.2 again: dot with a unit vector reads a coordinate.) No corner points, no extents — an infinite flat answer-surface to a one-line question.

A plane is not a thing, it's a question: a unit normal n and a number d — the plane is every point where dot(n, p) = d, and dot(n, p) − d is a signed distance answering "which side, and how far" in one subtraction.

Plane = { n: Vec3, d: number } with n unit length. The plane is the set $\{\,p : n \cdot p = d\,\}$; signedDistance(plane, p) = dot3(n, p) − d is positive on the side $n$ points toward. We call that side front.

Everything you do with planes is that subtraction plus a policy. classifyPoint buckets the signed distance into 'front' / 'back' / 'on' with an epsilon, because floats never land exactly on zero. planeFromPoints(a, b, c) manufactures a plane from three points: two edges span it, their cross is the normal — n = normalize3(cross(sub3(b, a), sub3(c, a))), then d = dot3(n, a). Walk the three points in the opposite order and the normal points the other way: winding is data, not style.

Now the killer app. A camera's visible region — its frustum — is just planes with normals pointing inward (six in 3D; four in the toy below). Wrap each object in a bounding sphere of center $c$ and radius $r$, and sphereInFrustum falls out of signed distance: a sphere is fully outside one plane exactly when signedDistance(plane, c) < -r, and any single rejecting plane is enough to cull. A sphere straddling a plane is still (partly) visible — cull only on < -r, not < 0, or objects will vanish while half on screen.

Frustum culling — six point-vs-plane checks per object — is how an open-world engine rejects most of the map before drawing a single triangle; it's the cheapest work a renderer ever does and the highest-leverage. And when a character's foot sinks through the floor, the fix is the same math run backwards: the penetration depth is the signed distance, so push the foot up by exactly that much.

2.6 Reflection: the bounce formula

You already own all the parts. A velocity $v$ hits a mirror with unit normal $n$. How much of $v$ points into the surface? Section 2.2: project $v$ onto $n$ — and since $n$ is unit length, that's just $(v \cdot n)\,n$. Subtract it once and the approach is cancelled — you get the grazing slide along the surface (that was reject, the Quake move). Subtract it twice and you push back out the other side as hard as you came in:

$$ r \;=\; v - 2\,(v \cdot n)\,n $$

That's reflect(v, n), with reflect3 the identical formula in 3D — the construction never mentions dimension. Two sanity checks worth carrying around: reflection preserves length (a mirror changes direction, never speed), and a grazing hit with $v \cdot n = 0$ passes through unchanged — nothing pointed in, nothing to remove.

Measure how much of v points into the surface — dot(v, n) — and remove it twice: once to cancel the approach, once to push back out; r = v − 2·dot(v,n)·n will light every shiny surface and bounce every ray for the rest of this course.

2.7 Capstone: shade a sphere with math alone

No geometry, no camera, no engine — just this module. Picture a unit sphere seen head-on, filling a disc of pixel coordinates with $x^2 + y^2 \le 1$. At each pixel the surface sits at $z = \sqrt{1 - x^2 - y^2}$, bulging toward you. And for a unit sphere centered at the origin, the position on the surface is the outward normal — already unit length, free of charge. That's sphereNormal(x, y): a Vec3 on the disc, null off it.

Lighting a matte surface is one dot product. With $L$ a unit vector pointing toward the light, Lambert's law says brightness is max(0, dot3(n, L)) — full strength facing the light, fading with the cosine, clamped so surfaces facing away read 0 instead of glowing negatively. That's diffuse(x, y, L).

The shine is Section 2.6. Bounce the incoming light — scale3(L, -1) — off the surface with reflect3, then ask how directly the bounce flies at the viewer sitting along $+z$: dot it with vec3(0, 0, 1), clamp, and raise to the 32nd power so only near-perfect alignment survives as a tight highlight. shadePixel(x, y, L) returns diffuse plus that specular term, and the widget clamps for display.

Look at what's on screen: a convincingly 3D sphere — no geometry, no camera, no engine. One dot product per pixel (plus a bounce for the shine). What you wrote is, in every way that matters, a fragment shader; Module 7 makes that official, running shaders like this across real triangles.

One thing this module's tools can't do: turn anything. The guards patrol on rails, the car's heading is an angle we integrate by hand, and rotating a whole world of points needs machinery we don't have yet. Rotation — and scaling, and shearing, and the single idea that unifies them — is a matrix, and matrices are Module 3.
