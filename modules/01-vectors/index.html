Vectors: The Atoms of 3D

Points, displacements, and the handful of one-line functions every engine is built on.

Open up any 3D engine — Unreal, Unity, the renderer inside Blender — and at the bottom of every call stack you find the same thing: little packets of two or three numbers being added, scaled and measured, millions of times per frame. Character positions, camera directions, surface normals, velocities, forces — all of them are vectors, and nearly everything an engine does with them reduces to functions short enough to fit in a tweet.

This module has you write that vocabulary yourself — add, sub, scale, length, normalize, lerp and their 3D twins — and from the moment each exercise passes, your implementation drives the animations on this page. A wrong sign here is not a red test row; it is a turret aiming backwards.

Axes: +X right, +Y up. Angles: radians, always — degrees appear only in UI labels. Vectors are plain objects — Vec2 = { x: number, y: number }, no classes — and functions never mutate their inputs: every operation returns a brand-new object.

1.1 The two faces of a vector

Here is the entire data structure this module is about:

type Vec2 = { x: number; y: number }

const v = { x: 3, y: 2 }

Those two numbers carry two different meanings. Read as a place, v is the point 3 units right and 2 up from the origin. Read as a move, it is the instruction “go 3 right, 2 up” — issued from wherever you happen to be standing. A position and a velocity have the exact same shape in memory; only the role differs.

A Vec2 is two numbers with two meanings — “a place” or “a move” — and which one lives in your head, not the type system.

Addition is componentwise: add(a, b) returns { x: a.x + b.x, y: a.y + b.y }. Geometrically that is tip-to-tail: walk the move a, then walk the move b from wherever a left you. The rule happily mixes the two meanings, too — point plus displacement is a new point — which is why the rover below can plant its flag at add(a, b) and trust the math to know where the journey ends.

position = add(position, scale(velocity, dt)) — better known as position += velocity * dt — is the beating heart of every game loop ever shipped. You are about to write the add half of it.

1.2 Subtraction: the arrow between

Subtraction is also componentwise — sub(a, b) returns { x: a.x - b.x, y: a.y - b.y } — but its geometric reading is the one you will use every single day: sub(a, b) is the arrow that carries you from b to a. Verify it with the function you just wrote: start at b, walk the arrow, and add(b, sub(a, b)) lands exactly on a. Its little sibling neg(v) flips a move into the exact opposite move.

sub(a, b) points from b to a — “sub points at the first argument” — and reversing it is the most common vector bug you will ever write.

The turret below aims along sub(mouse, turretPos): the arrow from the turret to your pointer. Get the order backwards and it will very confidently aim at the exact opposite of everything you do.

toTarget = sub(targetPos, myPos) is the most-typed expression in gameplay code: every homing missile, every AI that looks at the player, every camera that frames a boss starts by asking “what is the arrow from me to it?”

1.3 Scaling and length

scale(v, s) multiplies both components by one number s: the arrow keeps its direction and stretches by a factor of s. A negative s spins it 180° through its own tail — same line, opposite way.

How long is an arrow? Drop a dashed line along x and another along y and the arrow becomes the hypotenuse of its own staircase. Pythagoras does the rest: $\operatorname{length}(v) = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$. Its cheaper sibling lengthSq returns $x^2 + y^2$ without the root — square roots cost real time, and comparisons never needed them: to ask “is anything within 30 meters?”, compare lengthSq against $30^2$ and skip the sqrt entirely.

3D length is Pythagoras applied twice — the only new math in the third dimension is one more + z*z — and pros compare lengthSq to skip the square root.

Now the “applied twice” part. In the box below, the yellow floor diagonal is one right triangle lying flat: $\sqrt{x^2 + z^2}$. That diagonal then becomes the leg of a second right triangle standing upright, with the height as the other leg. The hypotenuse-of-a-hypotenuse is the space diagonal: $\sqrt{x^2 + z^2 + y^2}$. Adding a dimension added a term, not an idea.

That widget is this course's first 3D view — which means something just turned (x, y, z) into pixels. That something is project3(), and for the next four modules it stays a sealed black box: you may orbit it, but not open it. Real engines hand you the same box (they call it a projection matrix). Module 5 pops the lid, and you will write every line of it yourself.

velocity = scale(aimDir, bulletSpeed) turns a direction into motion. And the humblest scale of all — multiply every move by dt — separates games that work from the early classics that simulated per-frame and literally ran twice as fast on a 60 Hz machine as on a 30 Hz one. Meanwhile, range checks ship as distanceSq(a, b) < r * r — distanceSq is simply lengthSq measured between two points; you will write it in §1.4 — same answer, zero square roots.

1.4 Normalization: pure direction

Divide a vector by its own length — scale(v, 1 / length(v)) — and you get a unit vector: length exactly 1, direction untouched. That is normalize. Unit vectors are the currency of direction: multiply one by any speed and you get a velocity of exactly that speed, no matter how far away the thing you aimed at happens to be.

normalize strips “how far” and keeps “which way” — when characters move faster diagonally, the missing line is almost always a normalize.

The diagonal bug famously shipped: classic shooters fed the raw input (1, 1) — length $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$ — straight into movement, so holding forward+strafe was a real, tournament-exploited 41% speed boost (“strafe-running”). The fix was one normalize.

One landmine remains: normalize({ x: 0, y: 0 }). The length is 0, the division births NaN, and NaN infects every calculation it touches — one frame later your entire scene is invisible and nothing threw. Course policy, enforced by the tests: normalize of the zero vector returns the zero vector. While you are in there, distance(a, b) is just length(sub(a, b)), and distanceSq follows the same pattern. Watch what happens below when the missile catches its target exactly.

1.5 Lerp: the workhorse of animation

Linear interpolation answers “what is the point fraction t of the way from a to b?” Take the arrow between them and walk part of it: $$\operatorname{lerp}(a, b, t) = a + (b - a)\,t$$ At $t = 0$ you stand on a, at $t = 1$ on b, at $t = 0.5$ exactly halfway. You already own every piece: add(a, scale(sub(b, a), t)). Leave t unclamped — $t = 2$ keeps walking past b, and sometimes that extrapolation is exactly what you want.

lerp(a, b, t) = a + (b − a)·t — start at a, go fraction t toward b; every smooth slide, fade and tween reduces to this one line.

The famous trap is cam = lerp(cam, target, 0.1) per frame: each frame closes 10% of the remaining gap, so a 144 fps machine closes gaps far faster than a 20 fps one — same code, different game. The fix is $\operatorname{damp}(a, b, \mathit{rate}, dt) = \operatorname{lerp}\!\big(a, b,\; 1 - e^{-\mathit{rate} \cdot dt}\big)$: the exponential makes the total catch-up per second identical no matter how the frames slice it. Here, rate is the chase strength and dt is the frame's duration in seconds.

One more thing lerp buys you, free of charge: curves. Lerp between two lerps and the moving point sweeps a parabola — a quadratic Bézier, the primitive under every font glyph and easing curve you have ever seen.

1.6 Into the third dimension

Everything so far was 2D on purpose — every idea fit on a flat canvas. Now add the third number:

type Vec3 = { x: number; y: number; z: number }

And… nothing else changes. add3 adds three components instead of two. length3 is the box diagonal from §1.3: $\sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}$. normalize3 keeps the same zero-vector policy. lerp3 is the same one-liner, built from add3, sub3 and scale3. You will port all seven functions in one sitting, because each is a mechanical edit of code you have already written and tested.

The only new math in 3D is one more component — every function you wrote this module ports mechanically.

The comet below flies on your Vec3 functions — and reaches the screen through project3(), the black box from §1.3. Still sealed; still Module 5's problem.

1.7 Capstone: the particle fountain

Time to spend everything you built. A particle system is the “hello, world” of game physics: a pool of particles, each just { pos: Vec3, vel: Vec3, age: number }, launched from the origin and abandoned to gravity. You write the two functions that power the whole thing:

  • spawnParticle(speed) — a fresh particle at the origin, velocity pointing in a random direction with length exactly speed (random vector, normalize3d to a direction, scale3d to speed), age 0.
  • updateParticle(p, gravity, dt) — one physics step, returning a new particle: update the velocity first (vel + gravity·dt), then move the position using the new velocity, then add dt to the age.

Velocity first, then position with the new velocity — that ordering is semi-implicit Euler, and it is the difference between simulations that settle and simulations that explode.

You just shipped a physics integrator. It's small, but it's real: the loop you wrote — velocity gains acceleration, position gains velocity, time marches — is, at industrial fidelity, the same loop running inside Havok and PhysX. Every vector function on this page came out of your editor, and from here on the course builds with your library.

Two loose threads to carry forward. You can add vectors and scale them by numbers — but what could a * b possibly mean for two vectors? Module 2 gives the two useful answers, and between them they power lighting, field-of-view checks, surface normals and collision response. And project3() is still a sealed box — Module 5 opens it for good.
