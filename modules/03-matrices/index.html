Matrices: Transforming Space

Columns as basis vectors, composition, the homogeneous trick, Mat4 and TRS hierarchies.

Module 2 ended with an itch we couldn't scratch. You can measure angles with dot, manufacture perpendiculars with cross — but you still can't turn anything. Spin a turret, swing a door, orbit a moon: every one of those is "move every point of this object, all at once, consistently," and we have no tool for it.

That tool is the matrix. Forget the spreadsheet of numbers you met in school: a matrix is a recording of where space went. Play the recording into any vector and it tells you where that vector ended up. This module you'll build the whole machine — 2×2, then 3×3, then the 4×4 that every GPU on earth eats for breakfast — and finish by wiring a solar system out of nothing but matrix multiplies.

3.1 A matrix is where the basis lands

Two vectors carry the whole 2D plane on their shoulders: $\hat\imath = (1, 0)$ and $\hat\jmath = (0, 1)$, the basis. Every vector you've ever written is secretly a recipe made from them: $(x, y)$ means $x \cdot \hat\imath + y \cdot \hat\jmath$ — "walk $x$ copies of î, then $y$ copies of ĵ."

Now transform the plane — rotate it, stretch it, shear it. If the transform is linear (gridlines stay straight, parallel and evenly spaced; the origin stays put), the recipe survives: wherever $\hat\imath$ and $\hat\jmath$ land, $(x, y)$ lands at $x$ copies of the new î plus $y$ copies of the new ĵ. So the entire transform is pinned down by just two landing spots — four numbers. Those four numbers are a 2×2 matrix, and we store them column-major: Mat2 = [ix, iy, jx, jy], where column 1 (m[0], m[1]) is where î landed and column 2 (m[2], m[3]) is where ĵ landed.

Read the columns: column 1 is where $(1,0)$ lands, column 2 is where $(0,1)$ lands — and matrix-vector multiplication is literally $x \cdot \text{column 1} + y \cdot \text{column 2}$. That's the whole operation.

$$ \begin{bmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \end{bmatrix} \;=\; x \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} + y \begin{bmatrix} c \\ d \end{bmatrix} $$

That's why transformVec2(m, v) below is a two-liner built from Module 1's add and scale — no double loop, no mystery. Sanity check it in your head: feed in $\hat\imath = (1, 0)$ and the formula returns one copy of column 1 and zero of column 2. Multiplying a matrix by î just reads off where î goes.

A matrix is a coordinate system you can mail to someone. An animated game character carries 100+ bone matrices — one per joint, each saying "this is where my elbow's local space sits now" — rebuilt and shipped to the GPU every single frame.

3.2 The matrix factory

If a matrix is just "where î and ĵ land," then building one is just answering that question. Rotation by angle $\theta$ (counterclockwise, radians): î starts at angle 0 on the unit circle and swings to angle $\theta$, so it lands at $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$. ĵ starts at 90° and swings to $90^\circ + \theta$, which works out to $(-\sin\theta, \cos\theta)$. Write the two landing spots as columns and you're done:

$$ R(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \qquad S(s_x, s_y) = \begin{bmatrix} s_x & 0 \\ 0 & s_y \end{bmatrix} $$

Never memorize the rotation matrix — re-derive it in five seconds by asking where î and ĵ go: $\hat\imath \to (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$, $\hat\jmath \to (-\sin\theta, \cos\theta)$. And the determinant is the matrix's area-scaling factor — negative means the grid flipped inside-out.

The determinant det2(m) = m[0]·m[3] − m[2]·m[1] is an old friend wearing a suit: it's exactly Module 2's cross2(column 1, column 2) — the signed area of the parallelogram the two columns span. The unit square has area 1; after the transform its image has area $|\det|$, so every area scales by that factor. Rotations have determinant $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$ (they stretch nothing), scaling2(2, 3) has determinant 6, and a mirror has determinant −1: same area, opposite winding.

Angles are radians, here and everywhere in this course. rotation2(30) is not 30 degrees — it's 30 radians, nearly five full turns, and the numbers it produces will look like nonsense. Degrees exist only in UI labels (the θ slider below shows both).

rotation2(t * spinSpeed) is every spinning coin pickup you've ever collected, and scaling2(1.3, 0.7) snapping back to (1, 1) is squash-and-stretch, the oldest trick in animation.

Plant this flag now: non-uniform scale (sx ≠ sy) breaks surface normals. Scale a ramp 2× horizontally and its normal must rotate to stay perpendicular — pushing the normal through the same matrix gives the wrong answer. The fix (the inverse-transpose) arrives in Module 7; for now, just remember normals are not ordinary vectors.

3.3 Composition: chains read backwards

One transform is nice; pipelines are the point. "Rotate, then stretch" should collapse into a single matrix so you pay for one transform per vertex, not ten. That collapse is matrix multiplication: mul2(a, b) builds the matrix that applies b first and a second — the same inside-out order as f(g(x)), because $M_2 (M_1 v)$ hits $v$ with $M_1$ before $M_2$ ever sees it.

$M_2 \cdot M_1 \cdot v$ applies $M_1$ first — chains read right-to-left, exactly like $f(g(x))$. And they don't commute: rotate-then-stretch is a different animal from stretch-then-rotate.

And you already wrote the hard part. A matrix is "where the basis lands," so the product's column 1 is just b's column 1 pushed through a — call your own transformVec2 from 3.1 on each of b's columns and staple the results together. No triple loop required (though one works too).

Don't take non-commutativity on faith — take $S = [2,0,0,1]$ (stretch x) and $R$ = rot 90°: mul2(S, R) rotates the F upright then fattens it; mul2(R, S) fattens it first, and the rotation carries that fatness somewhere else entirely. The widget's swap button is that argument in one click.

3.4 The translation problem and the homogeneous trick

Here's the embarrassment: the most common transform in any game — move this thing over there — cannot be written as a 2×2 matrix. Look at the formula from 3.1: $M \cdot (0,0) = 0 \cdot \text{col1} + 0 \cdot \text{col2} = (0,0)$, always. A linear map must fix the origin, and translation's whole job is to move it.

The fix is the best trick in graphics. Glue a third coordinate onto every point: $(x, y)$ becomes $(x, y, 1)$. Your flat 2D world is now the plane $w = 1$ floating inside a 3D space — and in that bigger space, a perfectly ordinary linear map can slide your whole plane sideways. Which one? A shear: the 3×3 matrix below leaves î and ĵ alone and tilts the $w$ axis, so the higher you sit, the further you slide. At $w = 1$, you slide by exactly $(t_x, t_y)$:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & t_x \\ 0 & 1 & t_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x + t_x \\ y + t_y \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

A linear map must fix the origin — and translation's whole job is to move it. The fix: glue a 1 onto every point, lifting the plane to $w = 1$, where a plain 3D shear slides it sideways. Translation in 2D is a shear in 3D — that is the entire reason 3D graphics uses 4×4 matrices.

In code: translation2(tx, ty) returns a column-major Mat3 (nine numbers; the translation column lives at indices 6 and 7), and transformPoint2(m, p) computes $x \cdot \text{col1} + y \cdot \text{col2} + 1 \cdot \text{col3}$ — that trailing $1$ is the point's $w$. Now ask: what should happen to a direction, like "the gun barrel points northeast"? Sliding the gun across the map must not change which way it points. Give directions $w = 0$: the translation column gets multiplied by zero and vanishes. That's transformDir2(m, v) — same matrix, one different bit.

Points get $w = 1$ and move; directions get $w = 0$ and don't. Module 1's point/arrow duality finally gets a type system — and it lives in the matrix.

War story: transform a gun's forward direction with $w = 1$ — one flipped flag — and the translation gets baked into your "direction." Bullets spray toward world-origin-plus-nonsense, and only when the shooter stands far from the origin. This exact bug ships in real games. When aim drifts with position, audit your w's first.

3.5 The sandwich: do it somewhere else

rotation2 only knows how to rotate about the origin — that's the only point a linear map can't move. So how do you swing a door around its hinge at $p$? You don't teach rotation a new trick. You bring the problem to the tool: slide the whole world so the hinge sits on the origin, do the plain rotation you already own, slide everything back.

$$ M \;=\; T(p) \cdot R(\theta) \cdot T(-p) $$

Read it right-to-left like every chain: $T(-p)$ first (hinge to origin), $R(\theta)$ second, $T(p)$ last (carry it home). The acid test of rotationAbout(p, theta) is the hinge itself: it rides to the origin, ignores the rotation, and rides back — exactly where it started. If your hinge wanders, your sandwich is inside-out.

To do anything "somewhere else," carry it to the origin, do it there, carry it back: $T(p) \cdot R \cdot T(-p)$. Learn the pattern once and reuse it forever — spinning doors, orbiting moons, zoom-at-cursor, rotation about any 3D axis. It is all the same sandwich.

First you'll need mul3 — same column-pushing idea as mul2, one size up. Then rotationAbout is three matrices and two multiplies, built from your own translation2 and rotation2 (we provide mat3FromMat2 to lift the 2×2 rotation into a 3×3 with no translation).

3.6 To 3D: the Mat4

Everything you just built scales up without a single new idea. A 3D point becomes $(x, y, z, 1)$, a 3D direction $(x, y, z, 0)$, and the matrix becomes 4×4 — sixteen numbers, column-major, translation parked at indices 12, 13, 14:

$$ M = \begin{bmatrix} m_0 & m_4 & m_8 & m_{12} \\ m_1 & m_5 & m_9 & m_{13} \\ m_2 & m_6 & m_{10} & m_{14} \\ m_3 & m_7 & m_{11} & m_{15} \end{bmatrix} \quad \begin{matrix} \text{cols 0, 1, 2: where } \hat{x}, \hat{y}, \hat{z} \text{ land} \\ \text{col 3: where the origin went} \\ \text{bottom row: } (0,0,0,1) \text{ for now} \end{matrix} $$

A 4×4 is bookkeeping you already know: the upper-left 3×3 columns say where x̂, ŷ, ẑ land; the fourth column says where the origin went.

One genuinely new decision: rotation in 3D needs an axis. rotationY(theta) spins about ŷ, leaving it untouched while x̂ and ẑ circle in the ground plane. Our right-handed convention pins the direction: curl your right hand's fingers from x̂ around +y, and at $\theta = \pi/2$, x̂ has swung to $-\hat{z}$ while ẑ swings to $+\hat{x}$. Derive it the 3.2 way — ask where each basis vector lands — and the matrix writes itself. mul4, translation3, scaling3, transformPoint3 and transformDir3 are your 2D functions with one more column to carry.

3.7 TRS and the conventions minefield

Open any object in Unity, Unreal, or Godot and the inspector shows position, rotation, scale. Under the hood every one of those engines bakes the same matrix: $M = T \cdot R \cdot S$ — read right-to-left, the model is scaled in its own local frame first, rotated second, placed in the world last. The move/rotate/scale gizmo you've dragged a thousand times is a live TRS decomposition. Your trs(t, ry, s) below is the beating heart of every scene graph.

$T \cdot R \cdot S$ is the only order where each knob does what the editor says: scale never touches position, rotation never skews, translation lands exactly at $t$. Swap the order and the matrices contaminate each other — the widget's red ghost shows S·R·T scaling and spinning the translation itself.

Row/column-major storage and row/column-vector math are two independent choices — a 2×2 grid of possible conventions. Most matrix confusion on the internet is two people arguing from different cells of that grid, each correct in their own cell. This course (like WebGL and glMatrix) stores column-major and multiplies column vectors, $M \cdot v$, chains right-to-left. DirectX-tradition code multiplies row vectors $v \cdot M$, chains left-to-right, and its matrices are the transposes of ours. Neither is wrong; mixing them silently is.

Don't accumulate rotation by multiplying a stored matrix every frame (m = mul4(spin, m)). Float error compounds; after a few thousand frames the columns drift off unit length and out of perpendicularity, and your model slowly shears into modern art. Store the angle, rebuild the matrix fresh each frame — that's why trs takes parameters, not matrices.

Honest disclaimer: trs takes a single Y-axis angle because composing X/Y/Z Euler angles is a genuine trap — at certain combinations two axes line up and you lose a degree of freedom (gimbal lock). Real engines store rotation as quaternions and convert to a matrix at the last moment. That's Module 9; one axis is plenty to run a solar system.

★ Capstone: a solar system is a matrix sandwich

Three bodies. The sun spins in place: sunMatrix(t) is just your rotationY(t). The planet is a sandwich you already know how to read — $R_y(0.5t) \cdot T(6,0,0) \cdot R_y(2t)$: right-to-left, the planet spins on its own axis, gets pushed 6 units out, and the whole arrangement swings around the sun. And the moon is the punchline: it doesn't orbit a point, it orbits the planet's moving frame — moonMatrix(t) starts from planetMatrix(t) whole and appends its own little orbit, $R_y(3t) \cdot T(2,0,0)$.

Child world = parent world × child local. That one multiplication is the scene graph — every engine's transform hierarchy is this capstone with more bookkeeping.

Tank, turret, barrel: muzzleWorld = tankM · turretM · barrelM · muzzleOffset. The turret yaws in tank space, the barrel pitches in turret space, and the muzzle flash spawns in world space — four matrices, zero special cases. You'll write this exact chain in every game with an articulated anything.

Wrong answers here are gloriously visible: get the moon's multiply order backwards and it orbits the sun at planet distance, serene and completely wrong. Watch the trails.

Two loose threads, both deliberate. First: the orbit camera you've been dragging all module is itself a matrix — "where does the world sit relative to my eye" is just a change of coordinate system, and in Module 4 you'll build it. Second: that camera matrix needs to be undone (world-to-camera is the inverse of camera-to-world), and inverting a general 4×4 is miserable — but for the rotate-and-translate matrices you built today, the inverse is almost free. That beautiful shortcut opens Module 4.
